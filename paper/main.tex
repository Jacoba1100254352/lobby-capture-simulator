\begin{abstract}
This paper introduces the Lobby Capture Simulator, a Java-based agent simulation focused on lobbying, money in politics, regulatory capture, and anti-capture reform. Unlike a legislature-centered institutional simulator where lobbying is one modifier on votes, this model treats organized interests as the central strategic actors. Lobby organizations allocate finite budgets across direct access, agenda access, information distortion, public campaigns, litigation threats, campaign finance, dark money, revolving-door access, and defensive spending against reform. The first runnable slice tests whether meaningful anti-capture reform threats cause organized interests to shift from ordinary policy capture into defensive reform blocking, and whether bundled reforms remain effective after that adaptation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

Lobbying and regulatory capture are not merely legislative pressure terms. Organized interests can shape outcomes through campaign finance, public-information distortion, technical comments, litigation delay, procurement design, enforcement priorities, and post-government employment incentives. A useful simulation must therefore model lobbying as an influence economy rather than as a scalar attached to a bill.

The project is intended for comparative institutional analysis. The first priority is to make assumptions explicit enough that reform bundles can be stress-tested against adaptation and evasion. Empirical data are used as plausibility constraints for spending, disclosure, campaign finance, rulemaking, and procurement distributions, not as causal identification by themselves.

\section{Model Overview}

The current implementation contains lobby organizations, public officials, regulators, candidates, enforcement agencies, reform regimes, policy contests, influence arenas, and scenario reports. A policy contest may represent a statute, agency rule, enforcement priority, procurement decision, litigation posture, election pressure point, or anti-capture reform.

Each tick follows six steps:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A policy contest is selected from the scenario's contest mix.
  \item Lobby organizations choose a strategy and allocate finite budgets.
  \item Influence channels modify public support, public benefit, private gain, information distortion, dark-money influence, revolving-door influence, campaign finance influence, and litigation threat.
  \item The relevant arena resolves the contest under the active reform regime.
  \item Enforcement may detect and sanction capture attempts.
  \item Lobby organizations update strategy memory and issue-specific spending multipliers.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Actors and Channels}

Lobby organizations carry issue preferences, budget accounts, influence intensity, access capital, technical expertise, public campaign skill, information bias, astroturf skill, litigation skill, revolving-door network strength, disclosure avoidance skill, reform-threat sensitivity, and defensive multipliers. Current channels are direct access, agenda access, information distortion, public campaign, litigation threat, campaign finance, dark money, revolving door, and defensive reform spending.

Reform regimes include disclosure, real-time contact transparency, beneficial-owner disclosure, contact logs, lobbying bans, cooling-off rules, public financing, democracy vouchers, contribution limits, dark-money disclosure, public advocates, blind review, anti-astroturf controls, enforcement budgets, audits, sanctions, appeal delay, defensive-spending caps, administrative cost, false-positive cost, and constitutional challenge risk.

\section{Validation Plan}

Validation will proceed in layers. LDA data constrain lobbying budgets, clients, registrants, and issue distributions. FEC data constrain campaign finance concentration and outside-spending pressure. Federal Register and Regulations.gov data constrain rulemaking timing, docket volume, comment volume, and agency/topic mix. Voteview and govinfo bill status data constrain legislative arena baselines. Seattle democracy voucher and New York City public matching-funds data constrain public-financing scenarios. USAspending data constrain procurement exposure and recipient concentration \cite{ldaData,fecData,federalRegisterApi,regulationsGovApi,voteview,seattleVouchers,usaspending}.

\section{Next Work}

The next model increment should replace synthetic budget assumptions with calibrated distributions from LDA and FEC data, then deepen rulemaking comments into first-class docket objects. After that, the main research need is sensitivity and ablation reporting: enforcement strength, disclosure strength, public financing, cooling-off restrictions, and evasion freedom should be swept systematically before the paper makes strong comparative claims.
