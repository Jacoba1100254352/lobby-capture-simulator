\begin{abstract}
Regulatory capture persists not only because organized interests spend money, but because they can move influence across venues when reforms close visible routes. This article introduces the Lobby Capture Simulator, a Java-based agent simulation in which lobby organizations and funders allocate finite budgets across access, information distortion, public campaigns, litigation threats, campaign finance, dark money, revolving-door access, think-tank and association intermediaries, and defensive anti-reform spending. Public administrative data are used as distributional anchors and validation diagnostics rather than causal estimates. The first synthetic results show why reform evaluation should minimize total influence distortion: anti-capture systems can reduce observed capture while hidden capture survives through venue, messenger, financing, and intermediary substitution.
\end{abstract}
