\section{Motivation and Contribution}

Lobbying and regulatory capture are not merely legislative pressure terms. Organized interests can shape outcomes through campaign finance, public-information distortion, technical comments, litigation delay, procurement design, enforcement priorities, and post-government employment incentives. A useful simulation must therefore model lobbying as an influence economy rather than as a scalar attached to a bill \citep{stigler1971economicRegulation,grossmanHelpman1994,hallDeardorff2006,baumgartner2009lobbying,dalbo2006capture,deFigueiredoRichter2014,bertrand2014whom,carpenterMoss2014}.

The project is intended for comparative institutional analysis. Its design contribution is a transparent mechanism model of strategic channel substitution: when one influence route becomes costly, interests may preserve influence by moving money, expertise, messengers, or pressure into adjacent venues. The current prototype therefore treats reform evaluation as a governance-design problem rather than a single-channel deterrence problem. It asks whether disclosure, public financing, cooling-off, anti-astroturf, public-advocate, blind-review, meeting-log, procurement-firewall, comment-authenticity, and enforcement systems reduce total influence distortion or mainly shift influence into less visible forms.

The contribution is threefold. First, the model makes lobbying organizations and funders the central adaptive actors. Second, it connects campaign finance, rulemaking, procurement, litigation, revolving-door incentives, dark-money opacity, and defensive anti-reform spending in one state model. Third, it separates empirical distributional anchors from causal claims, so validation can compare simulated moments against public data without implying that the data alone identify reform effects. The article should therefore be read as a model-design and mechanism-checking contribution rather than an estimated treatment-effect study. It follows the tradition of transparent agent-based modeling, where empirical work first constrains ranges and failure modes before supporting policy estimates \citep{epstein1999generative,windrum2007empirical,grimm2010odd}.

\section{Stage-and-Channel Framework}

The model follows a stage-and-channel view of capture. Organized interests can influence electoral selection, legislative agenda setting, agency drafting, notice-and-comment rulemaking, enforcement, litigation, procurement, and implementation. At each stage, the available channels differ: campaign finance is valuable around elections, technical comments and ex parte access are valuable around rulemaking, litigation threats are valuable after an unfavorable rule, and procurement relationships matter during implementation \citep{mccubbins1987administrative,golden1998rulemaking,west2004formal,yackeeYackee2006,gordonHafer2005,kallaBroockman2016,ansolabehere2003littleMoney,baumgartner2009lobbying}.

The central behavioral assumption is channel substitution. When a reform raises the cost of one route, organized interests first try nearby routes that preserve donors, vendors, messengers, relationships, or policy objectives. Direct giving can become outside spending or dark money; registered lobbying can become advisory work; public comments can become sponsored expert submissions or litigation; procurement lobbying can become consulting or subcontracting pressure. The simulator therefore reports both visible channel mix and inferred hidden influence, because some reforms improve one disclosure surface while moving influence elsewhere \citep{dalbo2006capture,carpenterMoss2014,lapiraThomas2017,oecdLobbying2021,brennanDarkMoney2025}.

\section{Model Overview}

The current implementation contains lobby organizations, public officials, regulators, candidates, enforcement agencies, reform regimes, policy contests, influence arenas, and scenario reports. A policy contest may represent a statute, agency rule, enforcement priority, procurement decision, litigation posture, election pressure point, or anti-capture reform.

Each tick follows six steps:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A policy contest is selected from the scenario's contest mix.
  \item Lobby organizations choose a strategy, evaluate substitution pressure, and allocate finite budgets.
  \item Influence channels modify public support, public benefit, private gain, information distortion, dark-money influence, revolving-door influence, campaign finance influence, litigation threat, comment-record quality, and hidden influence.
  \item The relevant arena resolves the contest under the active reform regime.
  \item Enforcement may detect and sanction capture attempts.
  \item Lobby organizations update strategy memory and issue-specific spending multipliers.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Actors and Channels}

Lobby organizations carry issue preferences, budget accounts, influence intensity, access capital, technical expertise, public campaign skill, information bias, astroturf skill, litigation skill, revolving-door network strength, disclosure avoidance skill, reform-threat sensitivity, and defensive multipliers. Current channels are direct access, agenda access, information distortion, public campaign, litigation threat, campaign finance, dark money, revolving door, think-tank or association intermediaries, and defensive reform spending.

Reform regimes include disclosure, real-time contact transparency, beneficial-owner disclosure, machine-readable meeting logs, contact logs, lobbying bans, hard lobbying budgets, cooling-off rules with enforcement, public financing, democracy vouchers, public-interest representation funds, contribution limits, dark-money disclosure, public advocates, blind review, comment-authenticity rules, procurement firewalls, venue-shifting detection, anti-astroturf controls, enforcement budgets, randomized audits, sanctions, appeal delay, defensive-spending caps, administrative cost, false-positive cost, and constitutional challenge risk.

\section{Strategic Allocation}

The model treats lobbying as a budget allocation problem under institutional constraints. Clients first replenish lobby organizations when expected private gain, exposure, and secrecy needs justify additional spending. For client \(c\), lobby \(l\), issue domain \(d\), and contest \(t\), funding is stylized as

\[
F_{c,l,t} =
\operatorname{clip}\left(k P_{l,d} E_{c,t} S_d (0.35 + G_{c,d} + R_{l,t}), 0, F_{\max}\right),
\]

where \(P_{l,d}\) is lobby issue preference, \(E_{c,t}\) is regulatory, procurement, enforcement, or campaign exposure, \(S_d\) is the calibration issue scale, \(G_{c,d}\) is client private gain, and \(R_{l,t}\) is reform-threat funding pressure for anti-capture contests. Funding enters the lobby budget and is also recorded as a money flow with source type, disclosure lag, traceability, legal risk, reputational risk, and contest linkage.

Lobby organizations then allocate channel spending from their available accounts. Each channel weight combines the active strategy, issue preference, expected return memory, and evasion incentives:

\[
A_{l,j,t} =
\min \left(B_{l,j,t}, I_l P_{l,d} W_{s,j}(1 + M_{l,j})(1 + Q_{j,t})\right),
\]

where \(B_{l,j,t}\) is spendable channel budget, \(I_l\) is influence intensity, \(W_{s,j}\) is the strategy weight for channel \(j\), \(M_{l,j}\) is learned return memory, and \(Q_{j,t}\) is an evasion or reform-threat adjustment. Channel allocations update public support, agenda access, information distortion, campaign influence, dark-money influence, litigation threat, revolving-door influence, and defensive anti-reform pressure.

Rulemaking contests carry a docket. Comment campaigns are generated from public-campaign, dark-money, information-distortion, and intermediary spending. Anti-astroturf strength raises authentication and reduces synthetic comments; sponsor expertise and information bias affect technical-claim credibility. Intermediary spending can increase sponsored technical submissions and template saturation even when direct lobbying is restricted. The arena then resolves the contest using the resulting docket authenticity, template saturation, and information distortion.

Comment records are triaged before reporting. A docket is divided into a unique-information core, organized technical content, template saturation, and astroturf or misattributed comments. Duplicate compression, authentication confidence, review burden, procedural acknowledgment, and substantive uptake are calculated separately. This reflects the administrative-law design point that raw comment volume is a noisy salience signal, not a measure of evidentiary quality \citep{acusMassComments2021,nyagFakeComments2021}.

The substitution engine calculates a switch score:

\[
S_{l,t} =
E[I] + C_m + A_n + V_o - L_c - D_c - T_s,
\]

where \(E[I]\) is expected influence under the substitute route, \(C_m\) is messenger credibility, \(A_n\) is anonymity value, \(V_o\) is vendor and relationship overlap, \(L_c\) is legal cost, \(D_c\) is disclosure cost, and \(T_s\) is setup time. The result is not a one-for-one channel replacement. Reports summarize substitution pressure, influence preserved, hidden influence, messenger substitution, venue substitution, and net transparency gain.

Reform success is evaluated with a failure-aware diagnostic. Observed capture is the resolved contest outcome. Hidden capture combines hidden influence, preserved influence, messenger and venue substitution, defensive spending, private gain, policy distortion, procurement bias, and enforcement forbearance. Total influence distortion combines observed capture, hidden capture, policy distortion, procurement bias, comment flooding, technical rulemaking distortion, administrative burden, and preserved hidden influence. A reform that lowers observed capture while increasing hidden influence, hidden capture, total distortion, or substitution-failure risk is treated as a possible failure rather than a clean success.

Enforcement applies after contest resolution. Detection probability increases with audit rate, enforcement budget, sanctions, public visibility, and evasion legal risk. When detected, sanctions reduce capture effects and add an evasion penalty. This makes evasion valuable only when opacity gains exceed expected enforcement and disclosure costs.

\section{Resolution, Calibration, and Replication Design}

Each arena resolves contests by combining baseline contest vulnerability with arena-specific susceptibility, channel pressure, hidden substitution pressure, public backlash, reform controls, and stochastic noise. For non-reform contests, the implemented capture-pressure score is

\[
C_t = R_t + A_a + I_t + H_t - K_t(1 - L_t) - B_t + \epsilon_t,
\]

where \(R_t\) is contest-level capture risk, \(A_a\) is arena susceptibility, \(I_t\) is weighted information, campaign-finance, revolving-door, and litigation pressure, \(H_t\) is hidden substitution pressure, \(K_t\) is the active reform-control bundle, \(L_t\) is control leakage from hidden and preserved influence, \(B_t\) is public backlash under transparency, and \(\epsilon_t\) is bounded run noise. Capture is recorded only if the pre-audit score crosses the arena threshold and is not reversed by detection and sanctions. Anti-capture contests are resolved separately by comparing the public case for reform against defensive lobbying, litigation delay, and constitutional challenge risk.

The model is initialized from a fixed scenario catalog, a calibration profile, and deterministic source fixtures or normalized public-data snapshots. Randomness enters through contest sampling, channel-allocation variation, process detection, sanctions, and final contest resolution. Every report is generated with fixed seeds and records run counts, contests per run, Java version, source Git state, and calibration checksum. Report CSVs include Wilson intervals for binomial capture and reform-success outcomes plus multi-seed sample standard deviations for capture, hidden influence, and total influence distortion. Tables in the article report selected interval and robustness columns while the full report snapshots remain in the repository. The ODD-style model description in \texttt{docs/odd-model.md}, scenario catalog in \texttt{docs/scenario-catalog.md}, validation plan, source moments, substitution audit, validation summary, and calibration queue are bundled as supporting information with the submission package.

\section{Empirical Bridge}

The implementation separates empirical distributional anchors, synthetic findings, and speculative design recommendations. Empirical claims in this article are limited to normalized source moments and validation diagnostics. Synthetic findings are generated by the simulator under fixed seeds and scenario definitions. Design recommendations are hypotheses for institutional stress testing, not policy estimates.

Normalized LDA rows are intended to constrain issue funding scale, covered-official visibility, and disclosure lag. Normalized FEC rows are intended to constrain donor concentration, public-financing share, and traceability. Normalized Federal Register and Regulations.gov rows are intended to constrain docket size, comment authenticity, template saturation, comment flooding, and technical-claim credibility. Normalized USAspending rows constrain recipient and agency concentration. The revolving-door panel is expanded for mechanism testing but remains a tracked fixture unless a live export is configured. The parameter map in \texttt{data/calibration/parameter-map.csv} records the intended low, middle, and high ranges for disclosure timing, money concentration, dark-money traceability, comment manipulation, enforcement capacity, procurement concentration, revolving-door share, voucher participation, intermediary routing, and substitution. The source-moment report in \texttt{reports/source-moments.csv} separately records top-client, top-donor, traceability, public-financing, comment-record, procurement, and revolving-door moments so the paper can distinguish source distributions from simulated outcomes.

The committed snapshot is a closed-window reproducibility scaffold for the 2024 EPA/ENV slice, not a representative empirical panel. Report builds default to the committed normalized rows under \texttt{data/snapshots/2024-env/normalized/}, and exploratory runs can redirect calibration with \texttt{LOBBY\_CAPTURE\_CALIBRATION\_DIR}. The live runner records LDA, OpenFEC, Regulations.gov, Federal Register, and USAspending requests when keys and source access are configured, but the committed FEC panel is a national-party committee slice rather than a dark-money or public-financing panel, and the committed revolving-door rows are tracked fixtures. These inputs do not estimate the causal effect of a disclosure rule, voucher system, cooling-off ban, or procurement firewall. Dark-money, public-financing, intermediary, and revolving-door outputs are internal stress-test variables in this paper unless a future source panel directly anchors them. The inputs provide schema checks, plausible state-variable ranges, and warning signs for calibration. The default fetch workflow copies deterministic fixtures into \texttt{data/raw/} for snapshot preparation; optional \texttt{--live} modes normalize caller-provided CSV paths, URLs, or source-native API responses into the same schemas. The causal structure remains explicit in the model equations and arena rules.

\section{Validation Plan}

Validation proceeds in layers. First, parser and fixture tests ensure stable schemas. Second, \texttt{make source-moments} extracts direct distributional moments from normalized source rows. Third, \texttt{make validate} compares report metrics against broad benchmark ranges and the typed parameter map. Fourth, the same validation target writes a substitution audit that flags rows where observed capture falls but hidden influence, hidden capture, total distortion, or substitution-failure risk rises. Fifth, \texttt{make calibration-queue} classifies misses into model tuning, metric splits, direct source moments, scenario coverage, scale alignment, and benchmark review. Current validation is strongest as verification and plausibility screening. The calibration queue still identifies weak bridges for hidden influence, procurement bias, revolving-door influence, donor concentration, and comment compression; those entries bound the empirical claims of the paper. Later snapshots can add health, transportation, USAspending procurement panels, DIME, LobbyView, public-financing, outside-spending, and revolving-door source exports once the environmental slice is stable \citep{ldaData,fecData,federalRegisterApi,regulationsGovApi,seattleVouchers,usaspending}.

\section{Discussion}

The first results are mechanism checks rather than policy estimates. They show that anti-capture reform should be evaluated by asking four questions at once: whether observed capture outcomes fall, whether hidden capture rises, whether total influence distortion falls, and whether administrative burden or defensive mobilization creates new costs. The model is useful for stress-testing reform packages because it allows nominally strong rules to fail through substitution, administrative burden, weak enforcement, or defensive mobilization. The present scenario settings also reveal a limitation: some strong-reform rows sit in a near-saturated observed-capture region, so the strongest evidence is the substitution pattern and total-distortion diagnostic conditional on that region rather than a definitive reform ranking.

This framing fits a regulatory-governance audience because the unit of analysis is not a single vote or donation, but a design problem across institutional venues. Transparency systems, disclosure lags, public financing, comment authentication, meeting logs, public advocate offices, enforcement budgets, procurement firewalls, and revolving-door rules are modeled as interacting features of a governance architecture. The policy-relevant output is therefore not a single reform ranking, but a map of where influence moves when a rule binds.

\section{Limitations and Future Validation}

The main limitation is evidentiary rather than computational. The simulator now has reproducible scenarios, generated reports, source parsers, source moments, substitution audits, and validation queues, but the public-data bridge remains preliminary. The current 2024 EPA/ENV slice is useful for schema validation and distributional diagnostics, yet it remains incomplete for dark money, public financing, intermediary routing, and live revolving-door mechanisms. A stronger validation cycle would broaden the FEC panel beyond national party committees, add direct public-financing and outside-spending sources, replace the fixture-based revolving-door panel with a documented source export, and keep procurement concentration separate from simulated procurement bias.

The second limitation is uncertainty presentation. Report writers include Wilson intervals for binomial capture and reform-success outcomes and now report multi-seed standard deviations for capture, hidden influence, and total distortion. These robustness summaries are still finite-run diagnostics, not uncertainty intervals around an empirical target. The strongest defensible claim is that the model exposes reform-failure mechanisms that a single-channel lobbying model would miss.

The third limitation is calibration coverage. Some benchmark misses may reflect model parameters, while others likely reflect benchmark definitions that combine incompatible quantities. Additional empirical work would split direct source moments from simulated outcome moments, tune comment compression and authenticity against docket-level data, calibrate detection and sanction incidence against enforcement records, and anchor intermediary and revolving-door substitution with stronger source panels. These steps would move the model from a mechanism-checking article toward a calibrated policy simulation.
