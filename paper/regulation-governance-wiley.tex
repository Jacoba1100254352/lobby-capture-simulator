\title{Strategic Channel Substitution and Regulatory Capture: A Simulation Model of Lobbying and Anti-Capture Reform}
\author[1]{Jacob Anderson}
\authormark{ANDERSON}
\titlemark{STRATEGIC CHANNEL SUBSTITUTION AND REGULATORY CAPTURE}
\address[1]{\orgname{Independent Researcher}, \orgaddress{\country{United States}}}
\corres{Jacob Anderson}
\fundingInfo{No external funding is reported for this working draft.}
\keywords{regulation | governance | lobbying | regulatory capture | computational simulation | transparency | enforcement}

\abstract[ABSTRACT]{Regulatory capture persists not only because organized interests spend money, but because they can move influence across institutional venues when a reform closes one visible route. This article introduces the Lobby Capture Simulator, a Java-based agent simulation that treats lobbying organizations, funders, and influence channels as the central strategic system. Lobby organizations allocate finite budgets across direct access, agenda access, information distortion, public campaigns, litigation threats, campaign finance, dark money, revolving-door access, and defensive spending against reform. Policy contests are resolved across legislative, rulemaking, procurement, litigation, enforcement, campaign, and public-information arenas. The current implementation uses public administrative data as distributional anchors rather than causal estimates, then evaluates whether anti-capture reforms suppress influence, redirect it into adjacent channels, or improve observability while preserving hidden influence capacity. The first results show why transparency and enforcement should be evaluated as bundles: reforms can lower visible pressure while leaving organized interests able to substitute messengers, venues, or financing routes.}
